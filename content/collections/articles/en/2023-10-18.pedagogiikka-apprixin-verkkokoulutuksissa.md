        text: 'E-learning courses are characterised by the absence of a teacher or another person to explain things, ask questions, answer questions, guide progress, and verify competence. Because such a person is missing from e-learning courses, it is important to consider pedagogical perspectives when building them and to ensure that the course does not consist merely of a large mass of theory text to be read. When you create an e-learning course that is educationally coherent, participatory, diverse, and practical, employees, employers, and the entire workplace community all benefit.'

        text: 'Applying Knowledge'
  -
    type: paragraph
    content:
      -
        type: text
        text: 'Although pedagogy often involves talk of sharing knowledge, educational effectiveness does not mean just one-way transfer of information to an individual. In fact, anyone can be asked to share knowledge, but what matters is how that knowledge is shared. This is precisely where pedagogy comes into play. When knowledge is shared, it is important that it is also put to use. In e-learning courses, this means participatory tasks, where knowledge is not simply read and clicked through but rather applied. Knowledge is processed, internalised, and above all, learners consider what a particular piece of information means in practice. Such pedagogical challenging makes problem-solving more versatile and practical, and a little challenge also avoids underestimating the learner. In e-learning courses, for example, case tasks are an excellent opportunity to highlight common problems in the workplace and challenge the learner. In these tasks, a workplace situation or event is described, and the course participant is asked to react or provide a solution. The correct answer is always reviewed, so even if the wrong answer is given, the correct course of action with its rationale is revealed.'

        text: 'The Importance of Guidance'
  -
    type: paragraph
    content:
      -
        type: text
        text: 'A massive wall of text to read, hours of duration, and an extremely difficult competence test do not mean that it is a good e-learning course. Quite the opposite. Overly broad modules without activation, engagement, and visual elements turn the course into drudgery and are likely demotivating. When an e-learning course is instead divided into sections, defining objectives and reviewing learning outcomes also becomes easier. However, dividing into sections alone is not pedagogically sufficient. Because there is no instructor, the e-learning course must be clear in language and guiding in content. The technical implementation must not be an obstacle to fluency or content navigation, for example through excessive clicking or overly complex functionality. The purpose is to use technological solutions to clarify the e-learning course and improve its flow and logistics between sections. It is therefore essential that the course guides the individual in terms of both content and technical actions. In other words, the course participant must understand what the purpose of the course is, what its objectives are, how to progress through the course, where they currently are, what to do on each page, and when the course has been completed and the completion has been recorded.'

  -
    type: paragraph
    content:
      -
        type: text
        text: 'In online pedagogy, it is sometimes necessary to cover a great deal of content that could be explained orally in instructor-led teaching. In e-learning content production, one must "anticipate additional questions" – that is, mentally consider what questions the content might raise. A good rule of thumb is to stay on topic, but backgrounds, causes, consequences, or case examples can be expanded upon. Additional information can also be placed behind a link if reviewing it is optional. If you want all course participants to go through the additional information, it is extremely convenient to cover it in the form of a task.'

        text: 'As in pedagogy and project management, building e-learning courses also starts with objectives. It is therefore essential to consider what knowledge and skills a person who has completed the training should have. The course can only truly be built once the objectives are clear and defined. When e-learning courses are created for working life, the objectives emphasise the ability to apply and utilise what has been learned in practice and in one''s own activities. Achieving this goal brings not only knowledge and skills benefits, but when individuals are prompted to reflect on their ways of working while completing an e-learning course, they become aware of the significance of their own actions both as individuals and as part of the community. A competent, aware, and thinking individual is everyone''s advantage.'
