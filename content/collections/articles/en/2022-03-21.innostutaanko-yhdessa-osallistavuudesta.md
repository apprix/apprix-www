      -
        type: text
        text: ' A sense of participation is key when the goal is to motivate learning and ensure that what has been learned can be applied in one''s own work. However, few people actually know what the term means or how engagement relates to higher-quality learning.'

    type: paragraph

        text: 'When course tasks include engaging elements, the learner must think more deeply about their own behaviour. This in turn helps put what has been learned into practice.'

    type: paragraph

      -
        type: text
        text: 'Additionally, the learner''s commitment to the subject matter increases when they are given the opportunity to participate in building knowledge. In our clients'' e-learning courses, the importance of commitment is emphasised, as the course content often relates to various safety environments or, for example, competition law.'

        text: 'Thought experiment:'
      -
        type: text
        text: ' You need an e-learning course that comprehensively covers the different areas of information security. The company''s information security manager has excellent command of the intricacies of the GDPR regulation and has thoroughly studied the information security threats that may target your company. The information security manager has prepared a high-quality information security guideline that has consumed numerous work hours. The next step is to deploy this guideline to the employees working in the company.'
  -
    type: paragraph
    content:
      -
        type: text
        text: 'Now is the critical moment to invest in training, because the expert''s work will be wasted if employees do not follow the information security guidelines. One option is to conduct a live training session that ties both the trainer and trainees to a specific time and place. You could also create an e-learning course where the learner''s task is to read or listen to pre-digested facts. But would it not sound better to offer employees a course packed with tasks that challenges them to think about their own behaviour in everyday situations and their attitude towards information security threats?'
  -
    type: paragraph
    content:
      -
        type: text
        text: 'Tasks can serve as an orienting part of the course, where the learner is led to reflect on their own competence or learning objectives right from the start. On the other hand, you can use tasks to engage when you want to emphasise a specific area and ensure that the topic has been understood. If your company faces a heightened threat of data breaches, for example, you can put the learner in a challenging position by asking them to consider the consequences of opening attachments in a phishing email. Well-crafted tasks produce new perspectives and help the learner understand the magnitude of consequences their own actions can have.'
