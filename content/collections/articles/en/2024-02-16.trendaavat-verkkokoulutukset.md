        text: 'Value and well-being discussions are being held more than ever, workplace diversity and turnover have increased, information technology with its software, applications, and platforms has advanced in leaps and bounds, data traffic has grown, and recently the use of AI has also arrived in working life. With all of this, e-learning demand has recently seen a clear rise in the following themes: ethical conduct, sustainability, diversity and inclusion, and information security and data protection. In addition to growing general awareness and changes in working life, EU directives also affect some organisations'' operations and, consequently, their training needs.'

        text: 'A Closer Look at the Themes'
  -
    type: paragraph
    content:
      -
        type: text
        text: 'Ethical and responsible conduct guidelines and policies cover a broad range of areas in many organisations. They extend from tolerance and behaviour all the way to good competition and cooperation practices. Inclusion, diversity, and equality are emphasised, and sustainable development and environmental thinking are desired as part of everyday work. Many organisations have developed codes of conduct (Code of Conduct) to define good behavioural and competitive practices, and responsibility guidelines and reports demonstrate that the organisation is working towards responsible and sustainable operations and keeping up with developments. For example, ESG (Environmental, Social and Governance) and QEHS (Quality, Environment, Health and Safety) are highly sought-after responsibility training topics. The most recent influencer in sustainability themes is the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which came into effect on 1 January 2024, intended to get organisations to emphasise ESG activities even more strongly. Organisations may also set responsibility requirements for each other as a condition for cooperation, which may further increase the need for responsibility training.'

    type: paragraph
    content:

        type: text
        text: 'DEI training covering diversity, equity, and inclusion is also highly in demand. DEI stands for Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion. Among other things, internationalised working life and staff turnover lead to encounters with many different types of people, and the situation is easier for some than for others. In DEI practice, acceptance is emphasised, and difference is spoken of as diversity, which is seen as a strength in the organisation. Any related challenges are confronted and overcome through training, among other things. DEI training covers diversity as well as equal treatment and the inclusion and consideration of everyone; this comprehensive approach aims to create an observant, tolerant, and aware workplace community where everyone has equal value both as an individual and as part of the team.'

    type: paragraph

        type: text
        text: 'In addition to the themes mentioned above, ensuring, maintaining, and developing information security practices is visible in nearly all industries. The GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), which came into force several years ago, itself creates a need for data protection and information security training. The increase in hacking and cybercrime further adds to the need for information security expertise. Additionally, the rise of remote work may explain the growth in data protection training: for example, when working from a home office or in a cafe or train, data protection – such as confidentiality and the use of secure connections – must be maintained just as thoroughly as on company premises. AI also adds its own dimension to handling data and materials, requiring many companies to provide additional guidelines or clarifications regarding, for example, data sharing. It is worth reviewing and updating your organisation''s guidelines and training materials if AI is in use.'

        text: 'E-learning courses on the above topics emphasise both encouraging good practices and preventing misconduct. All of them also stress the obligation to report observed inappropriate behaviour. The impact can be seen in the organisation in many ways, such as improved workplace well-being and increased employee engagement and commitment, but also enhanced organisational reputation, competitiveness, and attractiveness. At the organisational level, responsibility, ethics, and information security also affect the success of partnerships and ultimately even the achievement of objectives. Preventing misconduct, on the other hand, saves organisations from many problems, such as illegal activities and their consequences, including compensation claims and even lawsuits. At the employee level, successful training affects the employee experience, workplace well-being, and satisfaction, all of which are directly connected to the smooth running of daily work.'

After all, these are extremely significant factors affecting the organisation''s operations, reputation, trust, and employees. Right from the start, it is essential to ensure that the goals and operating principles the organisation has set do not remain mere buzzwords, written guidelines, and wished-for practices. Instead, a way must be found to make goals and operating principles part of conscious, practical action. When e-learning courses are created on these topics, the primary objective is to develop the individual''s conscious thinking and behaviour.'
